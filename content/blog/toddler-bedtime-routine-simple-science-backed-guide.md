## Why Bedtime Feels Like a Battle (and How to Change That)

If you're spending 45 minutes just getting your toddler to stay in their bed at night — cycling through requests for water, one more story, and a mysterious need to check if the dog is okay — you're not alone, and you're not doing anything wrong. Toddler bedtime resistance is one of the most common sleep complaints from parents of children aged 1 to 4 years.

The reason it's so hard isn't defiance, exactly. Toddlers genuinely struggle to switch off. Their brains are absorbing enormous amounts of information every day, and transitioning from high alert to sleep requires a gradual wind-down that most toddlers can't manage on their own. That's exactly where a good bedtime routine comes in.

## What the Research Says

One of the most compelling studies on toddler bedtime routines was published in *Sleep* journal by Dr. Jodi Mindell and a team of international researchers. They tracked over 10,000 children across multiple countries and found that children with a consistent nightly bedtime routine fell asleep faster, woke less often overnight, and slept longer than children without one. The routine's content mattered less than its consistency — the same activities, in the same order, every night.

The American Academy of Pediatrics also recommends a predictable pre-sleep routine as one of the most effective tools for improving toddler sleep. The NHS echoes this, advising that children respond best to a calm, clearly signalled transition from waking life to sleep. When your toddler knows that bath is always followed by pyjamas, which is always followed by books, their brain starts releasing sleep-inducing hormones earlier in the process — essentially, the routine teaches the body to get ready for sleep.

## How Long Should a Toddler Bedtime Routine Take?

For most toddlers, a bedtime routine of 20 to 30 minutes hits the sweet spot. Shorter than that and there may not be enough time to genuinely wind down; longer and you risk your toddler getting a second wind, or the routine becoming so drawn-out that it feels unmanageable on tired evenings.

Starting the routine at roughly the same time each night is also important. Toddlers aged 1 to 3 generally do well with bedtimes somewhere between 6:30 pm and 7:30 pm. Going much later typically leads to overtiredness, which paradoxically makes it harder — not easier — to fall asleep.

## A Toddler Bedtime Routine That Works

Here's a framework you can adapt to your own family:

**1. Wind-down signal (5 minutes)**
Give a verbal warning about 15 minutes before the routine starts — something like "bath time in 15 minutes." This helps toddlers with transitions, who notoriously struggle when activities are stopped abruptly. Lower the lights in the living area and turn off screens at least 30 minutes before bed; research consistently shows that blue-light exposure delays melatonin release in young children.

**2. Bathtime (5–10 minutes)**
A warm bath does more than just clean your toddler. Research published in *Sleep Medicine Reviews* shows that bathing in warm water 1 to 2 hours before bed triggers a drop in core body temperature afterward, which signals the brain to increase melatonin production. Keep bath play calm rather than rambunctious — this isn't the time for splashing games that rev them back up.

**3. Pyjamas and sleeping bag or sleep sack**
Getting dressed in pyjamas is a tangible physical marker of the shift from day to night. At this stage, introduce a consistent comfort object if your toddler doesn't already have one — a soft toy or blanket that travels with them to the cot. Comfort objects are endorsed by the AAP for children over 12 months and can genuinely help with overnight settling.

**4. A small feed or milk drink (optional, seated)**
Many families include a small milk drink at this point. That's absolutely fine, but aim to offer it before teeth brushing and before your toddler is in their cot — you want to avoid feeding to sleep becoming the trigger for falling asleep.

**5. Teeth brushing**
Brief and non-negotiable. Building this in as a consistent part of the routine early means it becomes less of a fight as your toddler gets older.

**6. Two or three short books**
Reading aloud to toddlers has enormous developmental benefits, but it also serves a direct sleep function here — quiet, close, predictable interaction. Set a rule early: two books (or three), and that's it. When your toddler knows the number, the "just one more" negotiation loses steam.

**7. Into the cot or bed with a goodnight ritual**
A brief, warm, consistent goodbye — the same words or song each night — signals that you are leaving and this is the expected end of the routine. Keep this short. Longer goodbyes or repeated returns tend to escalate rather than soothe anxiety.

## Common Mistakes That Make Routines Backfire

The biggest pitfall is inconsistency. A routine that happens four nights out of seven does little for your toddler's sleep drive, because their brain never learns to rely on the cues. Even if some elements have to be abbreviated on difficult evenings, keeping the same sequence matters more than the duration.

Screens right up until bedtime are another common disruptor. A 2019 study in *JAMA Pediatrics* found that pre-bedtime screen use was associated with shorter sleep duration and later sleep onset in children under five. Even calming shows count.

Finally, watch the room environment. A room that is too warm, too bright, or not quiet enough can undermine even the best routine. The AAP recommends a room temperature between 68 and 72°F (20–22°C) for sleep. Blackout curtains and a white noise machine can make a meaningful difference, particularly for toddlers who are light sleepers.

## When to Seek Help

If you've had a consistent routine in place for three to four weeks and your toddler is still taking more than 30 minutes to fall asleep, waking multiple times a night, or waking before 5:30 am regularly, it's worth raising with your GP. In some cases, a paediatric sleep specialist or certified sleep consultant can offer tailored guidance.

Routines take time to work — usually one to two weeks before you see a real shift. But when they do click into place, they're one of the most sustainable improvements you can make to your family's sleep.
